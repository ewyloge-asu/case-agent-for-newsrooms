# Editorial standards

How this toolkit is built to survive your editor, your fact-checker, and your lawyer —
and where the humans stay in charge.

## The line we drew

**The agent does not write the story.** Not the draft, not the lede, not "a first pass
you'll fix." That boundary is a design decision, not a limitation we're working around:
a story generated by a model arrives with its judgment already embedded and its errors
already fluent, which is the most expensive kind of error a desk can inherit. Case
Agent's job ends where the writing begins — it delivers verified, sourced, organized
material; a person decides what it means and writes it.

What the agent *does* produce: database aggregates, ranked leads, case-file entries,
legal memos, footnotes on a human's draft, and dashboards. Every one designed to be
checked, not believed.

## The provenance contract

Every number that comes out of the analysis engine carries a provenance ID that points
to the specific source record it came from — down to the individual filing. One command
(`review.py "<id>"`) round-trips any claim back to the raw record. If a number can't be
traced, it doesn't ship. This is the property that makes AI-assisted findings usable at
all: verification doesn't require redoing the work.

## Anti-hallucination, mechanically

We don't rely on the model promising to be careful. The design removes the
opportunities:

- **Deterministic scripts compute; the model reasons.** Sums, joins, rankings, and
  statistics come from ordinary Python running over your records — the same answer
  every run. The model's job is deciding what to compute and what it means.
- **The footnoter flags, it never fills.** If no source supports a claim in a draft, it
  inserts a visible ⟨NEEDS SOURCE⟩ marker and a margin comment describing what was
  searched. Fabricating a citation is not in its vocabulary.
- **The legal checker under-claims by construction.** It quotes the current statute
  text and tests each element; its verdict scale tops out at "possible" and "genuinely
  unclear — ask a lawyer." It cannot output "this is illegal."
- **The report verifier re-checks transcription.** Numbers in written output are
  compared against the computed results file; discrepancies are listed, not smoothed.
- **The case file enforces evidence rules.** A lead cannot be marked *confirmed*
  without at least one source record attached; a dead lead must record why it died.
  A linter checks these invariants — they're not honor-system.

## Statuses your desk should adopt

Everything the pipeline produces is labeled by how much weight it can bear:

- **Verified** — checked against independent outside records; every number sourced.
- **Lead** — a pattern worth reporting out; explicitly not publishable as-is.
- **Flag** — a possible legal or integrity issue, framed as a question for counsel.
- **Closed** — checked and found empty, recorded with its evidence so nobody
  re-chases it.

Two disciplines the examples model, worth stealing: **disclosed ≠ independently
confirmed** (self-reported filings prove what was filed, not what happened), and
**pattern ≠ causation** (money arriving before a vote is a timeline, not a motive).

## Corrections

When the tooling itself gets something wrong — and it has: an amendment
double-count once tripled an outlier's dollar figure before it was caught — the fix
goes into the *tool*, the corrected method is recorded in the case file, and the
change is visible in version control. Same standard you'd hold a human methodology to.
